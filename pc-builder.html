Streamlining PC Building

How might we create a tool that assists Tweakers users, known for their expertise in building PCs, in a way that they find valuable?

The problem

How to help the experts?

Tweakers.net is a Dutch consumer electronics and technology-focused website well known for its community, editorial content, and price comparison tool, the Pricewatch.

In November 2022, I returned to Tweakers for an internship and my bachelor's graduation project. With Bo's support, I proposed a PC builder project to leverage Tweakers' tech focus and vast component database.

Tweakers users already having a high affinity with tech and PCs, the challenge central to this project was: How may we create a tool that assists Tweakers users, known for their expertise in building PCs, in a way that they find valuable?

The solution

Intuitive and informed component selection

Seamless interaction

Minimizes the amount of clicks in the user journey and helps users browse various components, while offering a complete overview of their PC build.

Error prevention

Utilizes the existing Tweakers Pricewatch database to check the compatibility between components and helps prevent potential user error with feed forward and feedback.

Showing what matters

Calculates and shows our power-hungry but thrifty users the price-performance at a glance along with other key information such as energy consumption

Potential outcome and future

At the time of writing the tool has not yet been implemented, and I am no longer employed or interning at Tweakers. However I have several hypotheses on how it would impact the business.

  • Increased website traffic by offering a localized experience tailored for Dutch PC builders
  • Increased usage of referral links as users finalize their PC build with partnered web shops
  • Higher engagement with ad-sponsored content such a PC component reviews and guides

On the other hand, the tool would still have to be developed and then maintained, and according to back-end development, data would have to be made usable for the tool. Lastly, there might be the occassional bug or edge case that may take up development time.

The process

Initial interviews and survey

Painpoints

Eager to know more about how Tweakers users build PCs, I reached out and interviewed five users. Four out of five users were all indeed very experienced, having built three, four or even more PCs in their lives.

...I completely overlooked that the power supply was meant for a small form factor PCs

But what surprised me the most is that despite their experience, they all had an anecdote to share about something they overlooked when they built their PC and the frustrating consequences thereof, such as the lengthy return processes and being unable to use the PC until they have the right part.

Priorities

Questioning how Tweakers users built their PCs, two words especially stood out to me as a trend: price and performance. It became clear why when I asked what they used their PCs for; gaming and the occassional 3D modeling, both very power-hungry use cases.

64.7% of Tweakers users prioritize price-performance over all else

Launching a survey to triangulate my interview findings, I received 425 responses that confirmed that Tweakers users have plentiful experience building PCs, and that they indeed prioritize price and performance most of all.

The competition

Comparing and analysing other tools, I saw a great amount of inspiration, but also things that could be better. What especially irked me about a tool like PCPartPicker is that you had to constantly jump from page to page as you built your pc.

On one hand dedicating an entire page for processors allows for in depth browsing, but I felt like there had to be a way to retain the overview.

I saw a potential solution on Newegg, where the tool took the form of a drawer on the side for the PC build as the user browsed components. Noting the similarities between Newegg's store page and Tweaker's Pricewatch, this design direction was very influential to me.

Feedback and narrowing scope

Wireframing early concept directions in FigJam provided a starting point and a great discussion piece when engaging with users and stakeholders.

Doing my rounds with stakeholders, I gathered several valuable insights as I converged towards a solution.

  • The editorial writers specialized in PCs provided valuable insights and feedback that helped guide the information architecture of the tool, and expressed their preference for Pricewatch integration as PC builders.

The processor is the first choice for many—With retailers even using them as loss leaders

  • Back-end development enlightened me about the feasability of a compatibility check using the existing Pricewatch data — and while feasible — I unfortunately learned it wouldn't be easy.
  • The head of the component testing lab made several valuable suggestions onhow to display a price-performance ratio, and confirmed the existence of standardized performance scores.
  • The product team exchanged valuable feedback and suggestions to enhance the project, for example encouraging me to focus on the core components to simplify the experience.

An additional user survey, yielding 445 responses, helped me hone in on the design direction, supported conversations I had with stakeholders, and validated some important assumptions.

Sketch, iterate, prototype

Utilizing the existing design system, I worked towards a higher fidelity design in Figma, balancing requirements and information density and incorporating stakeholder feedback with each iteration.

Once finished, I made the prototype using HTML, CSS and Javascript, allowing me create a non-linear testing environment with complex logic, which suits the nature of the tool well.

User testing

The first round of usability testing was mixed, overall the tool performed okay, but there were still a few critical points of improvement for the tool, thankfully these were all fairly remediable.

  • The tool was collapsed by default, significantly hurting its discoverability; participants would not even open the tool unprompted
  • The tool didn't prevent user error, but would only correct the users afterwards
  • The tool also didn't provide the participants with a next step after an error was made

Final iteration

Back to the drawing board, I sketched several ways to improve feed forward and feedback in the tool, as well as provide users a clear way to remedy potential errors.

But our expert users insisted on being able to see and pick all parts. Given that most incompatibilities can be resolved in more than one way (either swap part A or part B), it was logical keep all options open, as long as I made sure users are well aware of potential configuration issues.

In the follow-up tests the tool performed noticeably better, the second round of participants smoothly moving through the scenarios I prepared for them.

  • With the tool expanded by default, participants immediately discovered the tool and started using it
  • Partipants approached their scenario of building a PC much more smoothly thanks to the feed forward highlighting the correct choices.
  • Participants were much more positive about the tool in general, they found the user experience smooth and easy to use
  • The particpants left me with a few more recommendations to consider, such as being able to share the list with family or friends.

The tool provides clear information, is easy to use, matches Tweakers' style, and worked smoothly without any clutter or distractions

User scores

  • 3.9 out of 5 — For the tool's prevention and resolution of component incompatibilities
  • 4.1 out of 5 — For the tool's display of key component information
  • 4 out of 5 — For the clarity and usability of the tool

Closing

Final Thoughts

This was an invaluable experience as a real-world, end-to-end design project and I'm thankful to Tweakers for the opportunity. Hopefully the tool will indeed be implemented one day but I have to admit that it would require a lot of buy-in and effort to overcome technical contraints.

If time had permitted it I would've liked to dive deeper into and learn more about justifying the viability of the concept to stakeholders, especially from a business standpoint.

I also would've liked to expand the scope of the tool and explore more user journeys, for example envisioning of what comes after a user saves their component list, as that has unfortunately been left open ended.
