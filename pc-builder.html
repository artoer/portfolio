Tweakers

PC Builder Tool for Tweakers.net

Overview

About the client

Tweakers.net is a website that focuses on consumer electronics and technology, particularly on computers. Its revenue model is mainly through advertising and affiliate programs, such as the Pricewatch, a price comparison tool that allows visitors to find the cheapest electronics.

About the product

I designed a tool for Tweakers which helps the experienced users of the website build custom PCs. The tool is integrated into and leverages the existing features of Tweakers' Pricewatch functionality, a well-known price comparison tool in the Dutch tech community. The tool helps users by giving insight into the price-performance ratio of core components and important component information, by ensuring compatibility, and by giving useful feedback every step of the way. The tool also leverages the expertise of the Tweakers editorial team by offering recommendations.

My role

I independently managed the end-to-end design process of the tool for Tweakers as part of my graduation project. As part of the design process, I conducted user research, managed the client and stakeholders, defined requirements, brainstormed and wireframed, and finally made a clickable prototype and validated the design with users.

Challenges

The challenge is to design a digital interactive tool that helps experienced computer builders on Tweakers choose the right components, considering the various compatibility issues, user preferences, and budget constraints. I need to identify the users' needs and design a tool that can assist them in selecting the right components for their custom PC build.

Deliverables

A successfully tested and evaluated clickable prototype of the PC Builder tool, along with my conclusions and recommendations for future implementations.

Click here to view the protoype.

Strategy

My strategy involved conducting both a competitive analysis and interviews with potential users to gain insights on the strengths and weaknesses of existing solutions and better understand user needs and preferences. I also conducted a survey to validate my earlier research and obtain new insights, which confirmed that the target audience values the price-performance ratio most of all when choosing PC components.

Process

I brainstormed potential solutions using the how may we's and the Crazy Eight method which were used to generate several concepts. After more user research using a new survey and gathering feedback from stakeholders a concept direction was chosen and wireframed further. The wireframe was then made into a clickable, responsive prototype using HTML, CSS and Javascript to allow convincingly testing an endless number of combinations with users.

Iterations

Result

The tool was well-received during usability testing and when the prototype of the tool was made available to the Tweakers community, with a lot of praise towards the way the tool provides insight into the price-performance ratio of components.

During validation I identified several potential opportunities to improve the product in the future, from A/B tests that might be worth considering to features that can further enrich the user experience, such as the ability to share PC builds.

I would like to conclude this case study with the feedback I received from Bozhan, the senior UX designer at Tweakers and major stakeholder.

As a UX designer at Tweakers and the supervisor of the internship programme at the company, I would say that the PC Builder feature that Arthur designed and prototyped is complete and fulfils all requirements we set at the beginning of the project. The user journey is clear, there are helpful error messages and warnings along the way, components have clearly indicated empty and filled states, and the entire interaction has a clear end goal.

The success of the prototype I find is largely due to the rigorous research and testing process that Arthur applied to his project. The fact he designed and developed the tool iteratively, testing at each stage, meant that the decisions he took at each turn were validated and supported by solid qualitative and quantitative insights. The final evaluation of the prototype by the Tweakers community is a testament to this.

Lastly, the final version of the prototype Arthur leaves us with, and the list of next steps he has devised, together represent the first major milestone towards delivering the PC Builder feature to all Tweakers users. The handover is in fact so complete, that our development team is eager to get started on implementation before our product managers have even calculated its commercial viability.
