Tweakers

PC Builder Tool for Tweakers

Problem

Tweakers.net is a consumer electronics and technology-focused website, specializing in computers. Its revenue model relies on advertising and affiliate programs, including the Pricewatch, a prominent price comparison tool for electronics and important driver of website referals.

User interviews showed that Tweakers users, while experienced with building PCs, are still prone to mistakes and have a desire for more guidance when choosing computer parts.

As Tweakers boasts an extensive database of PC components and expert knowledge with it's testing lab and editorial team, I saw an opportunity to design a tool that fulfills these user needs.

My Role

As part of my graduation project, I independently managed the end-to-end design process for a conceptual PC Builder tool. My responsibilities included user research, stakeholder management, requirement definition, brainstorming, wireframing, creating a clickable prototype using HTML, CSS and JavaScript, and validating the design with users.

Challenges and Constraints

  • User Needs: Providing value to Tweakers users, an audience already experienced with computers.
  • Product Design: Defining the tool's appearance, positioning within the website, and user interaction.
  • Technical Viability: Ensuring the backend supports required functionality and availability of usable component data.

Solution

The tool for Tweakers is designed to assist experienced users in building custom PCs. Integrated into the existing Pricewatch page, the tool provides insights into price-performance ratios, component information, ensures compatibility, and offers recommendations from the Tweakers editorial team.

View the prototype

Key features

  • Provides valuable insights into what users care most about when choosing components: performance and value metrics, key specifications and power consumption
  • Safeguards users from potential mistakes by providing both feed forward and feedback during component choice, and guides user in resolving these issues or conflicts
  • Integration with Tweakers' Pricewatch, leveraging its extensive filters and offering the cheapest available price alongside the tool
  • Fill the tool with editorial recommendations at the click of a button, offering a good starting point for users or options to consider
  • User-friendly and described as clear and easy to use during usability testing

Impact

By making it easier for users to plan their custom PC builds on the Tweakers website, users will make more use of referal links as they explore their options or finalize their purchases at web shops, and engage more with ad-sponsored editorial content as they seek to further inform their purchase decisions.

Process

Discovery

Starting out with user interviews shed light on key aspects. It became clear that seasoned PC builders prioritize maximizing value for their budget. Interestingly, despite their experience, they also shared instances of making mistakes during the building process.

I followed up on my findings by conducting a survey on Tweakers, this helped me validate and expand on some of my earlier insights. For example by asking what users find the most challenging about choosing their components.

Crazy Eight and Figma

To begin synthesizing my learnings into a solution I employed sketching and brainstorming techniques like Crazy Eight, for instance to find effective ways to communicate the price-to-performance ratio to users and to find the layout, form and positioning that works for Tweakers.

I brought my ideas and sketches together in Figma, visualizing the first of many high fidelity iterations of the tool.

Sketching and wireframing helped highlight the challenges and constraints, such as trying to satisfy various user needs within limited screen real estate. My iterations focused on balancing information density and clarity, while also ensuring that the design as a whole aligned seamlessly with Tweakers.

When the design was ready and all possible interactions were accounted for, I leveraged my technical skills to make a prototype using code, providing a comprehensive non-linear testing environment.

User testing

With a finished prototype I moved on to user testing to gather initial feedback from participants, as well as discover the low-hanging fruit regarding usability and discoverability.

The iteration that followed performed significantly better in the follow-up test. The prototype was shared on the community forum where the reactions were broadly positive.
