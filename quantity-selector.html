Talpa eCommerce

Designing a mobile-first quantity selector for Actie van de Dag, a deal-focused e-commerce website.

Overview

For my internship at Talpa eCommerce, I worked on a project for their e-commerce website Actie van de Dag, where I was tasked with designing a solution for selecting the quantity of products a customer wants to purchase. Currently, this feature is only available as a dropdown in the shopping cart, but we wanted to also offer it on the product detail page, a step before it in the user flow. This was particularly important for tickets for leisure activities such as amusement parks and zoos, where customers often buy them in multiples to go with family or friends.

Research and Analysis

To start off, I began by creating screenshots of the current situation to better understand the existing user flow. It became clear that designing for mobile would be challenging, as we had a "sticky bottom bar" with the call-to-action and price of the product, leaving limited space for a quantity selector.

I also looked for inspiration from competitors, such as Amazon, who used a dropdown instead of a counter for quantity selection, while Coolblue had a counter. I then created rough sketches in Sketch, overlaying the screenshots of Actie van de Dag's product detail page on both mobile and desktop, and explored different design ideas. Given the limited space in the sticky bottom bar, I came up with the idea of integrating the price into the call-to-action, allowing space for the quantity selector, with the price dynamically updating as the quantity is adjusted.

Design Iterations

I presented the design variants to my team, taking into consideration worst-case scenarios where users may need to make additional choices such as color or length for products like curtains or concert dates. I also addressed concerns about the call-to-action looking crowded when displaying large numbers in the price. I showed various experiments such as a collapsible menu that opens from the sticky bottom bar, but it the team agreed hiding these under a toggle wasn't the way to go.

After receiving feedback from my team, we agreed that the best approach for mobile would be to have the price and call-to-action prominently displayed at the top of the page, allowing ample space for the button, price, quantity selector, and multiple choices. The bottom menu bar would then serve as an anchor link to scroll the user towards any options the user would have to select and the price.

Final Design

I refined my design based on the feedback and created a Sketch canvas with all the iterations and explorations. The final design solution includes a quantity selector integrated into the call-to-action button, with the price dynamically updating as the quantity is adjusted. On mobile, the price and call-to-action are prominently displayed at the top of the page, providing a seamless and intuitive user experience for selecting the quantity of products to purchase.

Challenges

Adding a quantity selector sounds like an easy assignment, but what made things complicated are some of the options some products offer, such as color or length of curtains, or the date of a concert. These had to remain easily accessible to the user. After many different experiments to add these options to the sticky bottom bar, it was decided to keep them at the top of the page to avoid visual clutter and maintain the ease of access for the user.

Another challenge was indeed the limited screen real estate, in particular the sticky bottom bar. Originally it contains the price of the product and a call-to-action, but not much room for anything else. Many different options were explored, such as moving the price to the call-to-action to make way for the quantity selector, or increasing the height of the sticky bottom bar. In the end the price was moved to the top of the page, and the quantity selector and the "add to cart" call-to-action remained.

Conclusion

Through this project, I was able to design an improved quantity selector feature for Actie van de Dag, enhancing the user experience by offering a more convenient and intuitive way for customers to select the desired quantity of products. I learned valuable lessons about designing for limited space on mobile devices, considering worst-case scenarios, and collaborating with colleagues to arrive at an optimal design solution.
